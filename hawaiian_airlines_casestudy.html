01

The Problem Hawaiian Airlines Brought to Deloitte

Hawaiian Airlines came with two interconnected challenges that were compounding each other. The first was a crew scheduling problem. Flight crews, pilots and flight attendants, were not being scheduled optimally, creating a cascade of operational and financial consequences.

Higher Labor Costs
Paying crew members for unproductive idle time and costly overtime that could be avoided with better scheduling logic
Scheduling Conflicts
Crew availability issues causing delays and cancellations, directly impacting customer experience and on-time performance
FAA Compliance Risk
Strict regulations govern crew rest periods. Poor scheduling increased exposure to violations and regulatory penalties
Crew Dissatisfaction
Unpredictable, inefficient rosters creating poor work-life balance, a retention risk in a competitive labor market

The second problem was structural. Hawaiian's System Operations Control Center, the nerve center managing all real-time flight operations, was running on legacy technology with manual processes, siloed data systems, limited forward visibility, and slow disruption response. When a weather event or mechanical issue hit, the SOCC couldn't respond quickly because it couldn't see the full picture.

The compounding effect: crew inefficiency created disruptions, and an outdated SOCC couldn't manage those disruptions effectively, so each problem made the other worse.

02

Why Deloitte Was Well-Positioned

Hawaiian Airlines was already a Deloitte client through NetworkPRO, Deloitte's proprietary asset for airline network planning and optimization. This existing relationship gave us three things a cold engagement never provides: established trust, existing access to Hawaiian's operational data, and credibility from demonstrated prior value.

I worked directly with the Managing Directors to structure the business case. My contribution spanned problem framing, hypothesis development, operational diagnostic structuring, ROI modeling, and C-suite presentation design.

In product terms, this is the retention and expansion motion. The hardest part of a new engagement is credibility. Having it already meant we could spend our energy on the actual problem instead of proving we understood airlines.

03

Crew Efficiency: The Diagnostic

To build the business case, we first needed to quantify what inefficiency was actually costing Hawaiian. I analyzed crew scheduling data, rosters, flight schedules, union agreements, and historical delay records, to identify and size four core inefficiency buckets.

4
Core inefficiency types identified across crew operations
3+
Route types analyzed: inter-island, long-haul, and mainland
5yr
NPV horizon modeled in the ROI business case

What we found: the scheduling algorithm Hawaiian used had been designed for a simpler route network and couldn't handle the complexity of their current operations, a mix of short inter-island hops, long-haul mainland routes, and international flights. Schedulers were compensating with manual overrides that solved immediate problems but created downstream conflicts. And because crew scheduling and flight operations ran in separate systems, changes to the flight schedule didn't automatically propagate to crew assignments.

Root cause: the system wasn't broken, it was built for a different version of the airline. Hawaiian had grown in complexity faster than its tooling.

04

SOCC Modernization: Four Workstreams

The SOCC diagnostic was structured across four parallel workstreams, each designed to produce both findings and actionable recommendations.

Process Modeling
Level-2 and Level-3 process maps of SOCC operations across flight monitoring, disruption management, communication coordination, and resource allocation. Documented what operators actually do minute-by-minute during normal and disrupted conditions.
Enterprise Architecture
Mapped all software systems supporting each SOCC function, documented data flows between systems, and identified integration gaps. The most critical finding: no single system had a complete picture of operations.
Organizational Analysis
Interviewed SOCC managers and operators, observed live operations, and collected metrics on disruption resolution times and manual handoff frequency. The most valuable insight came from observation, not interviews.
Business Case + Roadmap
Built a multi-year transformation roadmap, prioritized improvements by ROI and implementation risk, and developed a 5-year NPV model for the full modernization program.

Watching SOCC operators work during a real weather event was the most clarifying thirty minutes of the whole engagement. They were solving the right problems using the wrong tools, and they'd built workarounds so refined that the workarounds had their own workarounds.

05

The Solution We Recommended

The recommended solution combined three levers that addressed both problems simultaneously.

Process redesign: eliminate manual scheduling overrides by improving the algorithm's handling of Hawaiian's route complexity and making crew scheduling respond automatically to flight schedule changes. The goal wasn't replacing schedulers, it was giving them better inputs so overrides became the exception, not the norm.

Technology modernization: real-time crew scheduling integrated directly with flight operations, giving SOCC operators a single source of truth. Disruption management dashboards with forward visibility, so problems could be anticipated, not just reacted to.

Operational rules refinement: work within existing union agreements to design crew pairing strategies that maximize utilization while respecting rest requirements. This was the most politically sensitive piece, the solution had to be operationally better and contractually sound.

The framing we used with C-suite: this isn't a technology project. It's an operations transformation that technology enables. The investment is in getting the right information to the right people at the right time.

06

The Business Case We Built

The ROI model quantified projected savings across four levers against a clear cost structure, producing both a payback period and a 5-year NPV. The model had to be conservative enough to be credible and compelling enough to justify the investment.

Reduced Overtime
Better scheduling optimization directly reduces the frequency of overtime situations, one of the highest labor cost drivers
Reduced Deadheading
Positioning crew more efficiently cuts the cost of flying crew members as passengers to reach their next assignment
Improved Utilization
Higher ratio of paid-time-flying to paid-time-idle across the crew base, recoverable through better pairing strategies
Delay Cost Reduction
Fewer crew-related delays and cancellations reduces direct operating costs and customer compensation exposure

Against these savings, we sized the investment required: software licensing, implementation services, change management, and training. The model showed a payback period well within Hawaiian's planning horizon and a compelling 5-year NPV, enough to make the decision straightforward for a financially disciplined leadership team.

Outcome

$1M

The pitch landed. Deloitte Managing Director confirmed the engagement win. The crew efficiency diagnostic became Phase 1, with SOCC modernization as the defined follow-on program, the $5M+ opportunity we'd scoped in the business case.

What mattered wasn't just the revenue number. It was that the engagement was structured with a clear problem, a defensible business case, and a solution that Hawaiian's leadership could actually execute. That combination, problem clarity plus financial credibility plus operational realism, is what closes enterprise consulting engagements.

08

What This Taught Me About Product Work

1
Business cases are product specs.
Every element of a consulting pitch maps directly to product management work. Problem framing, user pain, solution design, success metrics, ROI, the language differs, the discipline is the same. The skill of translating an operational problem into a financial argument is identical to translating a user problem into a prioritization decision.
2
Existing relationships are distribution.
Hawaiian was already a Deloitte client. The NetworkPRO relationship gave us credibility and data access that a cold engagement never would have. In product terms: retention and expansion matter more than acquisition. The hardest thing to build is trust, and it compounds.
3
Observation beats interviews.
The most valuable findings came from watching SOCC operators work in real-time, not from structured interviews. Users describe what they think they do. Observation shows what they actually do. In both consulting and product, the gap between those two is where the real insight lives.
4
Good systems get outgrown.
Hawaiian's scheduling system wasn't badly designed, it was designed for a simpler airline. The problem was that the airline had grown faster than the tooling. This is a product lifecycle problem as much as an operations one. Products need to be revisited not just when they break, but when the context they were built for changes.
