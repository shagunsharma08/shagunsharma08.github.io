PM Case Study 001 B2C · Housing Tech / Civic AI 48-hour hackathon · March 2026

The 30-Second
Building Safety Answer.
LeaseGuard.

NYC renters spend 3+ hours researching whether a building is safe before signing a lease. I identified the problem, built a voice-enabled AI system that collapses that research to 30 seconds, and documented what it took to get it right.

01

The problem no one
had connected yet.

A renter trying to check whether a building is safe before signing a lease has to visit seven different government portals. HPD violations on one site. DOB permits on another. 311 complaints on a third. ACRIS property records somewhere else. Each with its own search interface, its own login, its own data format. No single view. No synthesis. No easy answer.

Most renters give up after the second site. They make a gut call based on incomplete information, or they ask a friend who rented nearby. One person I interviewed nearly signed a lease on an apartment with 47 open heat violations. She would have found out in January.

"I almost rented a place that had 47 open heat violations. I would have found out in my first winter. But nobody tells you to check this stuff until it's too late."

Renter, Brooklyn, Feb 2026

The government data is public. It has always been public. It is just scattered across seven agencies with no unified interface. The innovation was not the data. It was the integration.

The core insight: NYC already publishes everything a renter needs to make an informed decision. The problem is access, not data. Build the bridge.

02

Two users, one
broken system.

Through interviews with 8 NYC renters and 2 housing advocates, I found two distinct users who each had compelling reasons to want this, and neither had a tool that worked for them.

The renter is 23–45, about to sign a lease, with 24 hours to decide. Their current workaround is calling friends for anecdotes, skimming three or four low-quality websites, and making a gut call. What they want is simple: "Just tell me if this place is safe while I'm still on the viewing call with my agent."

The housing advocate works at a tenant rights nonprofit, Met Council on Housing, NYLAG, or similar. They spend 20+ minutes per consultation pulling data from multiple sources on behalf of renters. What they want is a tool they can hand renters for self-service, so they can spend their time on actual advocacy instead of data retrieval.

The difference: renters want speed, a decision in under 2 minutes. Advocates want scale, reduce repetitive research across hundreds of consultations. Same tool, different JTBD.

03

Why this
matters at scale.

NYC has 2.1 million renters. That is 65% of the city. Around 500,000 new leases are signed every year. Unlike San Francisco, which has Zillow-embedded safety scores, NYC has nothing. Renters resort to anonymous Reddit posts, anecdotal complaints, and luck.

Year 1 target: 50K–100K users (housing advocates plus informed renters). Year 2: 500K renters actively apartment hunting. Year 3 expansion to SF, LA, Chicago: 5M+ renters nationally, each city with its own equivalent open government APIs.

The model scales because the underlying approach, LLM function calling over public APIs, is replicable to any city that publishes building data. The data infrastructure is already there. We just connect it.

04

The architecture.
And the key decision.

The system has four layers. A voice and chat frontend built on Web Speech API and SpeechSynthesis. A Flask backend on Google Cloud that coordinates everything. The Gemini API using function calling to query all seven data sources. And NYC Open Data, seven public APIs covering HPD violations, DOB violations, 311 complaints, DOB permits, ACRIS records, fire violations, and health violations.

Voice / Chat UI (Web Speech API)
↓ HTTP POST: user question
Flask Backend (Google Cloud)
↓ Question + system prompt
Gemini API (function calling)
↓ Queries 7 NYC Open Data APIs in parallel
HPD · DOB · 311 · Permits · ACRIS · Fire · Health
↓ Synthesized, grounded answer
User gets: "350 W 42nd has 14 open HPD violations (3 Class C), 28 unresolved 311 complaints about heat and mold. Risk: HIGH."

The key architectural decision was embedding all seven API links inside the Gemini system prompt rather than hardcoding API calls in the Flask backend. This means Gemini reads the prompt, decides which APIs are relevant to the user's question, calls them via function calling, and synthesizes only what it retrieved. It cannot speculate. It cannot hallucinate. If the data does not exist, it says so.

Why this matters for product: changes to the data layer require editing a prompt, not deploying code. New city? Add its APIs to the prompt. New agency? Same. The integration layer is configuration, not engineering.

The frontend uses the browser's built-in Web Speech and SpeechSynthesis APIs. No third-party voice library. A renter can ask a question while standing outside a building on their phone and hear the answer read back in their language. Multilingual support (Spanish, Mandarin, Bengali) is planned for V1.1 and requires only pointing the SpeechSynthesis API to the right locale.

05

Before and after.
In concrete terms.

Before LeaseGuard: a renter visits data.cityofnewyork.us, searches for HPD violations by address, manually counts them and notes severity, goes back and searches 311 complaints on a different portal, checks DOB violations on a third, tries to compile the findings mentally, Googles the address for anecdotes, and makes a decision, usually with incomplete data and low confidence. Time: 3 to 5 hours. Confidence: low.

After LeaseGuard: the renter opens the site, asks one question in their own language, and gets an answer synthesized from all seven government sources in 30 seconds. The answer names which APIs were queried. It explains severity. It gives a risk level. It recommends next steps.

Before
7 portals · 3–5 hours · 1–2 sources checked · manual synthesis · low confidence · no risk framing
After
1 question · 30 seconds · all 7 sources queried · AI synthesis · sourced answer · explicit risk level

Three design principles drove this. Visibility first: the renter always sees exactly which data sources were checked. No black-box AI. Grounded responses only: Gemini cannot speculate, it must call real APIs before answering. And voice-first: a renter should be able to use this tool while standing outside the building on their phone, without typing a single character.

The constraint forced a discipline I think about often. We shipped the core thing. We deployed it. We got real feedback in 48 hours. If we had tried to ship the audit log and onboarding too, we would have shipped nothing well.

07

How I'd know
if this worked.

I defined four KPIs before building. Not after. The discipline of writing success metrics before you start building is one of the most useful habits I've developed, it forces you to be honest about what "working" actually means.

30s
Time to answer. Baseline: 3–5 hours of manual research.
7/7
APIs queried per response. Renters currently check 1–2 of 7 sources.
60%
First-visit activation target: first visit to first query.
85%
Post-query helpful rating. "Did this help you make a decision?"

The 60% activation target is the one I watch most closely. If fewer than 60% of first-time visitors ask a question, that tells me the onboarding is failing, they don't understand what the tool does or the voice input is not working in context. That is a UX problem, not a data problem.

08

Five things this
taught me.

1
Grounding is the hardest problem in AI products.
Without forcing Gemini to call real APIs through the system prompt, it speculates. With it, the output is trustworthy. For consequential decisions, housing, finance, health, the product is only as good as its grounding. Charm without accuracy is a liability.
2
Government data is an underused product surface.
NYC publishes everything. The seven agencies, the APIs, the violation histories, the permit records, all public, all queryable, no authentication. The innovation here was integration, not data collection. Before building data infrastructure, check what government already publishes.
3
Voice is not a nice-to-have for mobile-first products.
I added voice thinking it was a differentiator. Users told me it was the feature. "I don't want to type an address while standing outside the building." Voice made the product usable in the context where it actually matters. Interface choices are product choices.
4
Write success metrics before you build, not after.
Defining the four KPIs before the hackathon started meant every build decision had a clear test. Is this feature helping time-to-answer? Does it improve activation? Without pre-defined metrics, you build features that feel good instead of features that work.
5
Time constraints are a feature.
48 hours forced a prioritization discipline that a six-month roadmap never would have. We shipped what mattered, deployed it, and got feedback. The audit log and onboarding can be V1.1. The core thing needed to exist first. Ship, then iterate, in that order.
09

What's next.

V1.1 is focused on two things: multilingual support (Spanish, Mandarin, and Bengali, NYC's three largest non-English speaker communities) and a historical comparison feature so renters can track how a building's violation count has changed over time, not just see the current state.

The expansion thesis is straightforward. San Francisco, Los Angeles, and Chicago all publish equivalent building safety data through their own open data portals. The Gemini system prompt model means that adding a new city is an editing task, not an engineering sprint. Swap the API links. Update the prompt. Ship.

The housing advocate dashboard, aggregate stats across buildings, invite codes for nonprofits, bulk lookup, is the B2B layer that makes this commercially viable. Advocates are the distribution channel. Renters are the users. Building for both unlocks the network effect.
